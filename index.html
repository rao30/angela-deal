How it works, step by step

Each year, two houses.
A simple rhythm.

Each year you close on one pair of properties. For each pair, one house is bought traditionally (bank pays you), and the other you finance yourself ‚Äî like a private lender ‚Äî earning monthly payments.

Your money

What you actually
walk away with.

Every time a pair closes, you receive cash at the table. Plus, for each property you hold the loan on, you get a check in the mail every single month ‚Äî like clockwork ‚Äî until year 5 when everything is paid off completely.

The monthly payments are like a CD at the bank ‚Äî except instead of 0.5% interest, your "bank" is paying you back the full purchase price with zero fees. Pure principal, straight to you.

Why would I agree to 0% interest on my loan?

Because 0% interest is the fair trade for something very real you're receiving: a buyer who is going to pour money into these properties.

Renovations, repairs, updated kitchens and bathrooms, new appliances, fresh paint, landscaping ‚Äî all of that costs real money, and it all goes directly into properties that are still your collateral until the balloon is paid. Every dollar spent improving these homes increases their value, which means your security as the lender gets stronger over time, not weaker.

Think about it this way: a bank charges interest because it's lending money it could deploy elsewhere. You're not a bank ‚Äî you're a seller who was already planning to offload one property a year anyway. This deal gets all 8 sold in 4 years instead of 8, gets you paid for 9 years straight, and the whole time your collateral is being actively improved by someone with skin in the game.

The 0% isn't a gift. It's your price for delivering a buyer who invests in the properties rather than just collecting rent and letting them deteriorate. By the time the balloon comes due, these homes will be worth more than when you sold them ‚Äî and that's what guarantees you get paid.

When exactly does each balloon happen?
The 5-year clock starts from the date each pair closes ‚Äî not from a single fixed date. So the pair you sell in Year 1 balloons in Year 6. Year 2 balloons in Year 7. Year 3 in Year 8. Year 4 in Year 9. You'll receive four separate balloon payoffs, one per year from Year 6 through Year 9 ‚Äî which is actually great, because it spreads out the large paydays and the tax impact across multiple years.
What if the buyer can't refinance when the balloon comes due?
Then the balloon is in default and you have full legal recourse ‚Äî which means you can foreclose and take the property back, keeping everything paid to date, or negotiate an extension with better terms. In practice, a buyer who has been making payments for 5 years, raised the rents, and built equity has every incentive and ability to refinance. Banks love this deal profile.
What if I need more money in a given year?
That's a conversation we can have. The closing cash amounts are negotiable. If you need a larger payout in year 1, we can structure that. The framework is flexible ‚Äî what matters is that your total income across all years meets your needs, and we can move money around within the deal to make that work.
Why not just list all 8 on the MLS and sell normally?
You absolutely can ‚Äî and that might be the right choice. But consider: selling 8 investment properties traditionally means 8 separate closings, 8 sets of agents and fees, capital gains tax on all equity at once, and no guarantee of timeline or price. This deal gives you one trusted buyer, known prices, no agents, and the ability to spread the tax impact across multiple years. It's not for everyone ‚Äî but for someone who wants certainty and simplicity, it's hard to beat.
What happens to my existing mortgages?
On House 1 of each pair (the bank-financed one), the buyer's new bank loan pays off your existing mortgage at closing ‚Äî you receive the difference as cash. On House 2 (your seller-financed one), you use the cash you received from House 1's closing to pay off that property's existing mortgage yourself, leaving it free and clear. Then you hold it as the lender. Both mortgages are gone after each pair closes.
